Impact of Various Sports on a Running Training Plan

Using Aerobic and Anaerobic Load as a Proxy for Training Stress

Garmin’s training metrics provide a useful way to quantify the impact of any sport on your overall training. In particular, Aerobic and Anaerobic Training Effect (or “load”) measure how much a workout challenged your cardiovascular systems. These metrics are derived from heart rate data (and sometimes pace/power), estimating the Excess Post-Exercise Oxygen Consumption (EPOC) to gauge training stress . In simple terms, Aerobic Training Effect increases with sustained moderate-intensity efforts (prolonged elevated heart rate), while Anaerobic Training Effect captures the impact of high-intensity bursts (sprints or hard intervals) . Garmin (via Firstbeat Analytics) essentially uses a heart-rate-based model to accumulate training load for any activity, whether it’s running, cycling, or team sports . This means a hard game of football or an intense tennis match can register training load in the same way a run does, as long as you wear your device and get heart rate data. Aerobic and anaerobic load thus serve as a common currency to compare the strain from different sports. Research shows that such internal load metrics (like TRIMP – Training Impulse) can be applied across sports to sum up your weekly training stress .

Using these Garmin metrics is a good proxy for how non-running activities contribute to your fitness. For example, if a soccer match yields a high Aerobic Training Effect (say 4.0, “highly improving”) and notable Anaerobic Effect, you know it provided a significant stimulus to your endurance and speed systems . In contrast, an easy bike ride might only produce a low aerobic load, indicating a lighter impact. By relying on these measures rather than miles run, you can objectively account for all training. (Other proxies exist – e.g. rate of perceived exertion or power-based scores – but Garmin’s built-in load is convenient and well-calibrated). The key is that Garmin’s aerobic/anaerobic load lets you integrate different sports into your training plan quantitatively. Next, we’ll examine how various sports (especially those Garmin tracks) affect your running training, and how to adjust your plan based on those loads.

Endurance Sports (Cycling, Swimming, Rowing, etc.)

Cycling is perhaps the classic cross-training for runners. It provides an aerobic workout using similar leg muscles but without the impact of running. As a result, you can often do higher volume or intensity on the bike with lower injury risk. Studies have found that adding high-intensity cycling intervals enables runners to increase training volume without added stress on joints . In Garmin terms, a vigorous ride will generate a sizable aerobic load (and even some anaerobic load if it includes sprints or hill bursts). This can improve your endurance base and VO₂ max similar to running. In fact, cycling helps build aerobic capacity and leg strength while giving your knees a break . For integration, you might replace an easy run or recovery day with a bike ride, or use cycling on rest days to “flush out” your legs . If a long run is causing too much fatigue, a long aerobic ride could substitute partially – you’ll see a high low-aerobic load in Garmin’s metrics, indicating endurance work. Just remember that cycling is not weight-bearing; it won’t train your bones/tendons for running impact. So use it to supplement, not completely replace, key running sessions. Garmin will still count the bike’s training load toward your weekly total, so factor it in when balancing your program.

Swimming is another excellent endurance sport for runners, with the unique benefit of zero impact. An easy or moderate swim will register as low to mid aerobic load – great for recovery or adding aerobic volume without pounding. More intense swim workouts (interval sets in the pool) can also drive up your aerobic load and challenge breathing control. Interestingly, research showed that runners who did controlled breathing swim workouts improved their running economy by ~6% after 12 sessions . This suggests swimming can boost cardiovascular efficiency and upper-body strength, aiding your running indirectly. In your plan, swimming works well on a cross-training day or if you’re feeling too sore to run. Garmin’s devices (with a HRM-Swim or optical HR) will quantify the effort – you might get an Aerobic Training Effect number from a hard swim. Typically, use swimming for aerobic maintenance: e.g. instead of a 40-minute easy run, a 40-minute steady swim could provide a similar aerobic stimulus (Garmin might show, say, Aerobic TE 2.5 = “maintaining” fitness ). It won’t fatigue your legs, so it’s a useful way to add fitness while promoting recovery.

Rowing (whether on water or an indoor rower) and elliptical training are also in Garmin’s sport profiles and can contribute significantly to aerobic load. These activities engage large muscle groups (legs, core, and arms in rowing) and can drive heart rates high. A vigorous rowing session is essentially a full-body cardio workout; it builds both cardiovascular and muscular endurance . For runners, rowing can strengthen the posterior chain (glutes, hamstrings) and core while still logging an aerobic workout. If you spend 30 minutes on the rowing machine at a solid effort, you might see a training load number comparable to a tempo run of equal duration. Use rowing or elliptical on days when you want an aerobic workout but perhaps with some variety or less impact. Garmin will categorize an intense rowing workout’s load likely as “High Aerobic” if your heart rate is near threshold for extended periods. This contributes to your training in a similar way a steady hard run would. Just be mindful of specificity: while these build endurance, you still need running-specific training for your legs to handle running economy and impact.

Walking and Hiking are low-intensity activities that Garmin also tracks (often under “Walk” or “Hike”). These typically register as very low aerobic load (since heart rate stays relatively low). For a runner, walking is mainly a form of active recovery – it won’t significantly boost fitness but can aid circulation and recovery. A long hike in hilly terrain might raise your heart rate enough to enter zone 2 for a prolonged time, contributing some aerobic base training (Garmin could log a moderate load if it’s a brisk, uphill hike). You can treat hikes as supplemental easy days; just note the load is usually much lower than an equivalent-duration run due to lower intensity. Garmin’s Aerobic load scale accounts for your fitness level, so if you’re very fit, a slow hike may show minimal effect (e.g. TE 1.0 = “Minor benefit” ). In summary, endurance cross-training sports can effectively maintain or improve aerobic fitness with less orthopedic stress. Use Garmin’s load feedback to ensure these sessions are counted – for example, if you had to skip a run but did a hard bike ride, your 7-day Training Load in Garmin will reflect that so you’re not accidentally doubling up.

Team Sports (Soccer/Football, Rugby, Basketball)

Team field sports like soccer and rugby are highly demanding and can play a role in your running training – though they are less structured forms of exercise. Garmin watches have activity profiles for many of these (e.g. “Soccer” or you can use “Cardio” mode), which will track heart rate and output Training Effect. A key characteristic of sports like football (soccer) and rugby is that they involve intermittent high-intensity exercise: bouts of sprinting, acceleration, and agile movements, interspersed with periods of jogging or rest. This pattern provides a mix of aerobic and anaerobic stimuli. In fact, soccer is an excellent example of a sport that trains both endurance and speed. In a 90-minute match, an elite soccer player might run on the order of 10 km (about 6.2 miles), much of it at moderate effort, with dozens of short sprints on top . For a recreational player in a smaller game, you’ll still be jogging back and forth and performing quick sprints or intense efforts. This combination of sustained running and bursts of sprinting means soccer naturally delivers both aerobic conditioning and anaerobic interval work . Garmin’s metrics will typically show a high Aerobic Training Effect from the continuous running, plus a significant Anaerobic Training Effect if you did a lot of all-out sprints (for example, repeated breakaways or hard defensive efforts). Users have noted that a competitive soccer game can sometimes register very high training load — in some cases comparable to a hard interval workout, with aerobic TE and anaerobic TE both near the maximum of 5.0 (indicating an extremely taxing session) . In short, an intense soccer match can absolutely “count” as one of your hard training days for the week.

Rugby (especially touch rugby, which is often continuous play without heavy collisions) is similar. Rugby is generally considered a high-intensity, intermittent sport with insufficient rest between efforts . Players perform short, all-out sprints, directional changes, rucking or tackling efforts, then jog or pause briefly before the next play. This taxes the anaerobic system heavily and also keeps the heart rate elevated over the course of a game. A full 80-minute rugby match will push your cardiovascular system – but unlike steady running, it’s more about repeated high-intensity efforts and recovery. In training terms, that improves your ability to work at a high percentage of max effort and recover quickly, rather than pure steady-state endurance . Touch rugby (no tackles) typically involves continuous running and quick accelerations, which might skew slightly more aerobic than tackle rugby, but in both cases you get a robust anaerobic workout too. Garmin will log a rugby session much like a HIIT workout: expect a high anaerobic load if you’re sprinting a lot, and a moderate aerobic load from the overall duration at elevated HR.

Basketball and similar court sports also fall in this category. A game of basketball naturally produces high-intensity interval training through its play structure – sprinting down court, shuffling on defense, jumping, then brief stops in play. This forces your body to repeatedly recover from intense bursts, which is great for building anaerobic capacity and the ability to buffer lactic acid . It also elevates your heart rate for a sustained period (games often last 40–60 minutes of play time), contributing to aerobic conditioning. For runners, playing a sport like basketball can maintain leg speed and explosiveness, and the aerobic base to some degree. Garmin’s load might show, for example, an anaerobic focus if most of your weekly training was basketball and only a little low-intensity work.

Integrating team sports into a running plan requires some thought. These sports can substitute for a hard running workout, but you have to ensure recovery and balance. If you play an intense soccer match on Wednesday, that likely serves the role of a speed or tempo session for the week – your legs and cardiovascular system have been thoroughly worked. It would be wise to treat the next day as a recovery day (Garmin might even recommend 2 days recovery for a very hard game, based on the high load). Many runners who play soccer count the game as their weekly interval training or tempo run . In practice, that could mean: instead of doing a separate track workout that week, use the soccer match as your quality session, then focus other days on easy runs and your long run. The aerobic load from the sport contributes to your weekly volume, but keep in mind that the aerobic stimulus, though significant, is a bit more fragmented than a steady run. One runner noted that relying solely on soccer for training meant fewer straight continuous miles, so they ensured they still did a weekly long run and one other run to keep up pure running endurance . This is a smart approach: enjoy the sport, log its training benefit, but don’t neglect run-specific long efforts especially if training for a distance race.

Also pay attention to training load balance. Garmin’s Training Load Focus feature divides your load into three categories: low aerobic, high aerobic, and anaerobic . Team sports tend to pile load into the high aerobic and anaerobic buckets (lots of intense efforts), while possibly under-serving the low aerobic range (the truly easy, long-duration efforts). If your Garmin reports an “Anaerobic focus” (meaning a large chunk of your recent training is very intense work), it will suggest balancing that with more low-intensity volume . In other words, after a hard game, make sure your next run is an easy one – a gentle jog to build base endurance and promote recovery . Conversely, if you somehow are doing only low-intensity work, Garmin might flag a “High aerobic shortage” or “Anaerobic shortage” and recommend some faster workouts . The takeaway is to use these sports as part of the plan, not the entire plan. Enjoy the fitness gains from them (soccer will absolutely help your VO₂ max and lactate threshold due to the intense intervals ), but ensure you complement them with the steady aerobic runs that build a strong base. And on weeks when you have multiple games, be careful not to stack too many other hard runs – let the games themselves drive your high-intensity training, and do your running mileage at easier effort to avoid overload.

Racket Sports (Tennis, Padel, etc.)

Racquet sports like tennis, padel, squash, or badminton are another category of Garmin-tracked activities that runners might engage in. These sports are typically stop-and-go in nature – short rallies or points with brief pauses – which makes them another form of interval-type exercise. The intensity can vary widely depending on skill level and how competitive the play is. Generally, a singles tennis match involves quick sprints, lateral shuffles, and short recovery periods between points, keeping your heart rate in a moderate to high zone for an hour or more. Padel, which is usually played doubles on a smaller court, tends to have slightly lower movement demands than singles tennis, but still gets your heart rate up through bursts of activity. Studies on padel have shown that players sustain about 70–80% of their max heart rate on average during a match, indicating a solid moderate-intensity cardio workout with intermittent peaks . In practical terms, that’s like spending an hour in a vigorous fartlek workout – your heart rate hovers around a tempo effort for stretches, with spikes when you scramble for a ball and brief rests after points.

From Garmin’s perspective, a racket sport session will mainly contribute to aerobic load (for the sustained elevated heart rate) and some anaerobic load for the explosive efforts. If you play a hard 90-minute tennis match, you might find your Garmin showing an Aerobic Training Effect in the “improving” range (for example, 3.0–4.0, meaning it improved your aerobic fitness) and possibly an Anaerobic Effect if you did a lot of intense rallies. One avid tennis-playing marathoner reported that in an hour of tennis, their heart rate might average around 120 bpm with bursts into their tempo or even VO₂max zone during intense rallies . They treated tennis days as cross-training, sometimes shortening or skipping a running workout if the tennis session was particularly taxing . This matches the principle of listening to your body: tennis may not feel like a structured “workout” but it can be quite strenuous.

Integration into a run plan: Racket sports can be counted as cardio cross-training. If you have a weekly tennis game, consider it similar to doing a medium-intensity interval run or hill workout that week. It will maintain your aerobic fitness and leg turnover, but you should be aware of what it doesn’t do: it’s not continuous running, so it won’t fully replace an easy mileage run or a long run for building pure endurance. Therefore, use it to supplement your running, not entirely replace key run sessions. For example, if you normally run 5 days a week and you want to add some variety, you could run 4 days and play tennis on the 5th day as an aerobic cross-train day. Or if you’re very busy, you might replace one of your harder run workouts with a competitive tennis match – achieving a similar training load stimulus while having fun. Just be cautious scheduling it right before or after a big run workout: your legs might be more fatigued from all the lateral movements and quick sprints than a Garmin load number alone would indicate (Garmin focuses on cardiovascular load, and may not fully capture muscle fatigue from, say, lots of lunging in squash). Allowing an easy day after a high-intensity match is wise.

One advantage of these sports is mental freshness – they’re fun, so they can stave off burnout from running every day. They also engage different muscles (lots of lateral movement, quick reflexes). This can improve your agility and strengthen stabilizer muscles that pure forward running might not hit. In running terms, that might translate to better balance and injury resistance (since we’re avoiding injury focus, we’ll just say it makes you a more well-rounded athlete). Garmin will include your tennis/padel sessions in its 7-day load and recovery calculations, so if you see “You need 36 hours recovery” after a hard match, take that into account before your next hard run. In summary, tennis and padel provide a moderate aerobic stimulus and some high-intensity benefit; count them as quality cross-training, adjust your run schedule for recovery, and ensure you still get sufficient run-specific mileage in your week.

Strength and High-Intensity Gym Workouts

Garmin also tracks activities like Strength training, HIIT workouts, and Cardio classes. These aren’t “sports” per se, but many runners include them in training, so it’s worth noting their impact. Pure strength training (e.g. weightlifting sessions) usually doesn’t elevate heart rate enough to create a large aerobic load – you might notice a strength session barely moves the Training Effect needle on your watch. For example, a 45-minute gym session of heavy lifting might come out as Aerobic TE 0.5 (no real aerobic benefit) and perhaps Anaerobic TE 1.0–1.5 if there were short bouts that spiked your HR. This does not mean strength work isn’t valuable – it absolutely is for running performance and injury prevention. Studies show that adding strength training (targeting legs and core) improves running economy – essentially you use oxygen more efficiently, run faster with less effort . However, Garmin’s load metric is focused on cardiovascular load, so it won’t give you “points” for muscle stress. Therefore, you have to integrate strength training by feel and routine: typically 1–2 short strength sessions per week for runners is beneficial . You might schedule these on the same day as an easy run or after a key run, so that your hard days are hard and easy days remain easy (a common approach to avoid constant moderate fatigue).

HIIT or circuit training classes (sometimes logged with Garmin’s “Cardio” profile or similar) will show up in training load more than pure strength, because they keep your heart rate high. For instance, a CrossFit WOD or a bootcamp class with minimal rest can produce a substantial aerobic and anaerobic load, akin to an interval workout. If you do a 30-minute HIIT session that leaves you drenched in sweat, Garmin might give it a high Training Effect (e.g. Aerobic 3.5, Anaerobic 2.5, plus a sizable load number) – reflecting a strong stimulus to both systems. Treat these sessions like you would a hard running workout in your plan. That means don’t do them the day before a race or a long run, and if you do a heavy leg HIIT day, expect that your legs may need recovery before performing high-quality running repeats. The good news is these workouts can improve your anaerobic capacity and power, translating to better top-end speed and fatigue resistance for running. They also build functional strength. Garmin’s load focus might categorize a lot of these workouts as “High Aerobic” or “Anaerobic.” Ensure, again, that you balance them with low-intensity training. If your week is full of CrossFit, soccer, and hard runs, you might be skewed too far toward intense effort – Garmin would likely show an Anaerobic focus and advise adding more low aerobic sessions to balance recovery .

So, while strength and HIIT don’t directly pile up aerobic miles, they contribute to your fitness in ways your Garmin may only partially capture. Incorporate them for the benefits (stronger muscles, higher anaerobic threshold), but adjust your running plan around them. For example, if Tuesday is a hard track workout in your marathon plan but you also have a CrossFit session on Wednesdays, you may want to move one or ensure one is very easy – two back-to-back maximal efforts can lead to excessive fatigue. Use the Garmin recovery time guidance as a check: if that CrossFit class gives you a 24-hour recovery suggestion and your training plan calls for a hard run 12 hours later, consider tweaking the plan (either shifting the run or doing it easier).

Adjusting Your Running Plan to Include Other Sports

Bringing it all together, the key to integrating sports into a running program is balancing the training load and allowing for recovery, just as you would with runs. Here are some guidelines based on the research and Garmin data:
	•	Count the Load, Don’t Double-Up: Use Garmin’s Exercise Load or Training Effect from your sports activities to inform your plan. If your watch assigns a large load score to a sport session (e.g. 300-400 from a soccer game), recognize that as equivalent to a hard run. Adjust the week’s plan by reducing or skipping another intense workout. In essence, let that sport session replace a scheduled run workout of similar intensity. This prevents overtraining. For example, if your marathon plan called for a mid-week tempo run but you played an intense 90-minute soccer match on Tuesday, it’s reasonable to treat the soccer as your tempo effort for that week . You’d then do easy running on the following days and still do your long run on the weekend. By respecting the training load from other sports, you ensure you’re not unintentionally stacking hard workouts.
	•	Leverage Garmin’s Recovery Time and Status: After each activity, many Garmin watches display a recommended recovery time (based on the accumulated load/EPOC). If you play a hard match and Garmin says “Recovery: 2 days,” that’s a strong signal that the session was equivalent to a very hard run effort. Use this info when scheduling runs. It doesn’t mean do nothing for 2 days, but it suggests that any training in the next 48 hours should be light. Similarly, Garmin’s Training Status and Load Focus features can alert you if you’re overdoing it or unbalanced. For instance, if your 7-day load is way above your long-term average (Garmin might label you as “Strained” or give a high acute load warning), consider scaling back intensity until you’re in a better range. Firstbeat’s research (which underpins Garmin) emphasizes keeping the ratio of acute to chronic load in check to avoid burnout – meaning incorporate new sports gradually and don’t suddenly add five games a week on top of running. Gradual integration is key.
	•	Maintain a Balanced Intensity Distribution: As noted earlier, ensure your training has a healthy mix of low, mid, and high intensity work . Other sports often skew to higher intensity (because they’re competitive and stop-start). To counterbalance, make your running sessions around them predominantly easy. For example, if you have a weekly basketball game (high intensity), plus a track workout, that’s two very hard days. The rest of your runs that week (and cross-training sessions) should be easy aerobic mileage or recovery activities. Garmin’s Load Focus can guide you here by showing if you have a “Low aerobic shortage” – meaning you need more easy runs to support all the hard work . Remember, aerobic base (built by easy mileage or long slow runs) is what allows you to handle intense workouts and races. So if sports are giving you plenty of anaerobic stimulus, you can afford to convert some planned speed workouts into easier base runs. Conversely, if your cross-training is mostly cycling and swimming (aerobic but low intensity relative to running), you might need to keep some high-intensity run sessions to build speed.
	•	Plan the Week with Sport in Mind: It’s helpful to schedule your running plan around known sport days. If your football matches are on Sundays, perhaps make that the end of your week with a rest or very easy run on Monday after. Or if you play tennis every Friday, consider using Friday as a cross-training day in the plan and do your long run Sunday rather than Saturday to allow a day in between. Many coaches recommend the “2 hard days a week” principle for recreational runners – you can count your sport day as one hard day, and a quality run as the other. Everything else should be mostly low intensity. In terms of frequency, a rough guideline from coaches is: if you run ~4–5 times a week, you can safely replace one of those days with cross-training (and still improve) . If you run only 3 times a week, you might cross-train 2 other days to build fitness while giving legs a break . Always listen to your body – if you’re carrying a lot of fatigue (elevated resting HR, poor sleep, etc.), consider dialing back either the running or the extracurricular sports temporarily.
	•	Acknowledge Muscle Fatigue and Specificity: One thing Garmin’s load numbers don’t directly account for is the localized muscle damage or soreness from different activities. For example, a game of rugby might leave your upper body and core exhausted from tackles, or a squash match might make your quads and calves sore from lunging – even if the aerobic load is akin to a run. So adjust your training not just from the cardio perspective but also the orthopedic one. If your legs are shot after playing basketball on asphalt, doing interval repeats the next day is unwise even if Garmin’s recovery time didn’t seem huge. No focus on injury was requested, so we won’t dwell on injury risks, but suffice to say mixing in sports means you must stay flexible with your run plan. It’s okay to swap days or take an extra rest if needed. The variety can actually make you stronger (and stave off mental burnout) as long as you allow recovery.
	•	Use Cross-Training to Your Advantage: The upside of integrating other sports is you can maintain fitness during periods when running might not be optimal (e.g. a niggle or just needing low-impact days). Garmin’s data can reassure you that you’re still on track. For instance, if you replace a 10-mile easy run with a 40-mile bike ride, your Garmin might show a similar training load contribution for that day – you’re keeping your aerobic engine working. Down the line, this will pay off in your running performance. Many runners find that engaging in sports like soccer or cycling give them an aerobic boost and leg strength that translates to strong running, as long as they also do some running to keep specificity. The best endurance athletes often have a broad base – you’re building that by being multi-sport.

In conclusion, Garmin’s aerobic and anaerobic load metrics offer a practical framework for folding various sports into your running regimen. They confirm that exercise is exercise – your heart and lungs don’t know whether you’re on a track or a soccer field, they just respond to the workload. By monitoring those metrics, you can ensure your overall training load is appropriate and adjust your running plan accordingly. Prioritize the “main” training elements for your running goal (e.g. long runs for a marathon, speed workouts for a 5K), but intelligently slot in your games of touch rugby, football, padel, or gym sessions as part of the plan rather than as recovery afterthoughts. When done right, these sports will enhance your running – improving your aerobic capacity, strengthening ancillary muscles, and keeping you motivated – all while reducing monotony. Just maintain the balance (as Garmin would say, a “balanced” load gives all-around fitness benefits ) and you’ll reap the rewards of a multi-sport approach in your running performance.
