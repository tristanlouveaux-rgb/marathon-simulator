Target Heart Rate Zones by Runner Type and Workout

Heart Rate Training Zones and Runner Fitness Levels

Training with heart rate (HR) zones allows you to target specific intensities for different workouts. Importantly, these zones can shift depending on a runner’s fitness level. Novice runners typically reach their lactate threshold at a much lower percentage of their max heart rate (MHR) than elites, meaning a beginner’s “threshold” effort might occur at only ~55–65% of their MHR, while an elite’s threshold occurs around ~90–95% MHR . In other words, well-trained athletes can sustain a much higher fraction of their max heart rate aerobically before hitting their limit, whereas less-trained individuals start accumulating lactate at a lower HR . This is why novices must train at relatively lower heart rates (as a % of max) for the same effort level, especially on easy runs, to avoid crossing into anaerobic intensity too soon . As fitness improves, the heart can sustain higher intensities, and the HR zones “widen.” For example, elite marathoners can hold ~85–90% MHR during a race, whereas a novice might hit their threshold and fatigue if they tried to sustain even 80% MHR for long .

Heart rate zones are commonly divided into 5–7 ranges from very light to maximal intensity. These zones correspond to physiological markers (like aerobic vs. anaerobic metabolism) and are often defined as percentages of a reference HR. There are a few ways to calculate your HR zones, each with pros and cons:
	•	% of Max Heart Rate (MHR): The simplest method. You estimate or test your maximum heart rate and define zones as percentage ranges of MHR (e.g. Zone 2 might be 60–70% MHR) . Pros: Very simple and requires only age (for estimation) or a maximal test. Most fitness trackers default to this method. Cons: It’s generic and can be inaccurate for individuals unless you know your true measured max HR . Formulas like “220 − age” can be off by 10–20+ beats for many people . So, purely age-based zones often misjudge your actual intensity. Still, it’s a fine starting point for beginners if no better data is available .
	•	Heart Rate Reserve (Karvonen formula): Incorporates your resting HR to personalize zones. Formula: Target HR = HR_rest + (HR_max − HR_rest) × %Intensity . This uses the range between your resting and max HR (your HR reserve) rather than max alone. Pros: Accounts for individual differences in resting HR (indicator of fitness), making zones more tailored . For example, 70% intensity by HRR usually corresponds to a higher actual HR than 70% of MHR would, if you have a low resting HR . This method “further personalizes your training zones given that resting heart rate varies from person to person” . Cons: You need to know your true max HR (which might require a field test) and measure your resting HR consistently . Slight day-to-day changes in resting HR (due to stress, fatigue, etc.) can affect the formula a bit. Also, like %MHR, it doesn’t directly account for lactate threshold differences.
	•	Lactate Threshold Heart Rate (LTHR): Bases zones on your lactate threshold HR – roughly the highest HR you can sustain for ~20–30 minutes. This usually requires a lab test or a tough field time-trial to determine . Pros: Highly individualized to your fitness. Since threshold correlates with performance capacity, zones pegged to LTHR are very accurate for serious training . For example, coaches like Joe Friel define zones as percentages of LTHR (Zone 4 ~94–99% of LTHR, etc.) . This method inherently adjusts for whether you’re a novice or elite (elite runners naturally have higher LTHR relative to max). Cons: Requires a hard test or lab measurement to find your LTHR . Beginners may not have this data or be comfortable doing a 30-minute time trial. Also, threshold HR can improve with training, so you need to re-test periodically. But if you have the data, this is often considered the gold standard for setting zones for experienced runners.
	•	Hybrid or Advanced Methods: Some systems combine LTHR and MHR or use additional breakpoints (like Aerobic Threshold, VT1, VT2) to fine-tune zones . For instance, a coach might use your LTHR for middle zones but also ensure Zone 5 is capped near your actual max HR. Pros: Can provide a nuanced zone map (e.g. 7+ zones) that reflects both your sustainable threshold and your anaerobic capacity. Cons: More complex and usually only needed for elite athletes or the highly data-driven . Most recreational runners don’t need 10 different zones.

In practice, all these methods can guide training effectively if applied consistently. Research notes that no system is perfect or “one-size-fits-all,” since day-to-day physiology varies and zone boundaries are somewhat arbitrary on a continuum . The key is to pick a method that fits your experience and tools, then stick with its framework for clarity. For example, a beginner might start with simple %MHR zones, whereas an intermediate runner training for a goal race could do a field test to set LTHR-based zones .

HR Monitoring: Chest Straps vs. Smartwatches

No matter which formula you use, accurate measurement is crucial. Chest strap HR monitors (ECG-based) are considered the gold standard for exercise, whereas optical wrist sensors (common in smartwatches) can lag or err, especially during intense workouts. Studies have found wrist monitors can deviate by 20–40 bpm from true HR during exercise (while chest straps stay within a few beats) . In other words, a glance at your watch might show 160 bpm when you’re actually at 180 bpm, or vice-versa – a big issue if you’re relying on that number to stay in a target zone . For steady easy runs, a good smartwatch may be “good enough,” but in intervals or hill repeats with sudden changes, the chest strap will track your real HR much more reliably . Experts advise serious athletes who train by HR zones to use chest straps for precision . If you only have a wrist HR, be mindful of its delay/inaccuracy: use it as a rough guide alongside RPE. Many runners use the “talk test” or RPE to double-check intensity (e.g. if your watch says you’re in an easy zone but you’re breathing hard, trust your body over the device). The bottom line is that HR-based training is most effective when your data is accurate – so choose the monitoring method that suits your needs for accuracy versus convenience .

Target Heart Rate Ranges for Workouts (Novice vs. Elite)

Below we translate workout types (with given RPEs) into target heart rate zones. We provide approximate HR ranges using the methods above, noting where a novice might differ from an elite. If you’ve determined your lactate threshold HR (LTHR), use that as an anchor – we’ll note approximate percentages of LTHR. If not, you can use % of max HR or % of HR reserve (HRR) as noted. Remember that novices will be toward the lower end of ranges (they can’t sustain as high a % of max), whereas elites may hit the higher end. All HR numbers assume you’re using a reliable monitor and are properly rested and hydrated (fatigue or dehydration can elevate HR abnormally on a given day).

Easy Runs (RPE 3 – “foundation miles”): These should feel very comfortable – you can chat in full sentences. Target roughly Zone 1–2 intensities, which is about 50–70% of max HR . In terms of HRR, that’s around 50–60% of your HR reserve. If using LTHR, keep easy runs well below threshold – roughly <75% of LTHR (many coaches use an LTHR zone1 cap ~80% LTHR) . Novice runners may need to stay at the lower end (~60% MHR or even less), since their lactate threshold might only be ~60-65% MHR . In fact, beginners are often cautioned to keep easy runs “slow enough” – some guidelines say ~60–65% of MHR or below . This ensures the effort remains purely aerobic. Elite or highly fit runners can do easy runs at, say, 70% MHR and still be far below their threshold (because their threshold is ~90% MHR) . An elite’s easy pace might fall closer to 70% MHR (~75–80% of their LTHR) and still feel like RPE 3. Bottom line: easy runs are in the light/aerobic zone – keep HR roughly in the lower half of your aerobic capacity. If you find your heart rate creeping toward 80% MHR on an “easy” day, back off the pace (for many people, that indicates you’re working too hard for a recovery run).

Long Runs (RPE 5 – moderate effort but sustainable): The weekly long run should also be predominantly aerobic. Typically this is Zone 2 (light to moderate) intensity. Aim for roughly 60–75% of max HR during most of the long run. Coaches like Jack Daniels suggest ~70–75% MHR as an optimal long run effort for experienced runners, to maximize aerobic development without undue fatigue . In practice, many runners will see heart rate drift upward slightly on a long run; it might start near 65% and end closer to 75% as fatigue sets in. Novice runners should err on the side of caution – one guideline is to cap long-run HR around 60–65% of MHR for beginners . For example, a 30-year-old with ~190 max should keep long-run HR ≤ ~123 bpm in early training . This ensures the pace stays truly conversational. Advanced runners can handle the higher end of Zone 2; their long runs might average ~70% MHR comfortably, and even approach 75–78% MHR by the end if it’s a harder long run. In terms of LTHR, stay at ~80% of LTHR or below for the bulk of the run (well-trained athletes’ aerobic threshold (~LT1) is often ~75–80% LTHR). Long runs are about building endurance, not speed, so keep the effort in check. As one source puts it, you should “finish feeling like you could run farther,” not exhausted. Heart-rate data backs this up: in one example, a seasoned runner’s 12-mile long run averaged 70% MHR (felt “comfortable, not lazy”) . If your HR is spiking higher, ease off – the long run should be primarily fat-burning and aerobic.

Threshold / Tempo Runs (RPE 7–8 – comfortably hard): These runs are meant to be at or near your lactate threshold, the fastest pace you can sustain for a prolonged time (~20–30 min in a race or training). The target HR is around 85–90% of max HR for a trained athlete in a tempo run . By definition, your threshold HR ≈ 85–90% MHR if well-trained , and you aim to hold that steady. Novice or less-trained runners have a lower threshold relative to their max – possibly only ~70–80% MHR . So a beginner’s “tempo” might occur at 75% MHR and feel quite taxing for them. It’s okay if that seems low; it simply reflects a lower fitness level. The idea is to train near your threshold, whatever it may be. If using LTHR, by definition you’d target ~95–100% of LTHR for a true threshold workout (e.g. a 20-minute tempo at LTHR) . In practice, many tempo runs are done a few beats per minute below true LTHR to avoid early fatigue – roughly 90–95% of LTHR (or “high Zone 3 to mid Zone 4” in some systems) . Another way to frame it: threshold runs usually land in Zone 3–4 depending on zone scheme – about 80–90% of HRR or ~80–90% of MHR. For example, an intermediate runner might do a tempo at ~85% MHR (if their max is 180, that’s ~153 bpm). This effort corresponds to the point “where talking in full sentences is no longer possible” – you might get out a short phrase at a time . In short: aim for the HR you can hold at a hard steady effort for ~30 minutes. As fitness improves, that number (threshold HR) will rise. Well-trained runners may even push ~92–93% MHR at threshold intensity , while beginners might be near 80%. Use your lab or field test if available; otherwise, approach it as “comfortably hard” and check that you end the workout with HR near the expected zone.

VO₂max Intervals (RPE 9 – max aerobic intervals): VO₂max workouts involve hard intervals (often 2–5 minutes each) at about the intensity that elicits your maximal oxygen uptake. Heart rate will be very high, approaching your maximum by the end of each interval. Target ~95–100% of MHR during the work intervals . In practice, your HR may start lower and climb, often peaking in the final 30 seconds of a rep. For example, an athlete might see their HR go from 85% up to ~98% MHR over a 3-minute interval. If using HRR, this is ~90–100% of HRR (Zone 5). Everyone, novice or elite, will be near their personal max HR in a true VO₂max workout, because that’s the goal – to stress the upper limit of aerobic energy systems. One caveat: very short intervals (≤60s) may not allow HR to fully spike to max by the time the interval ends, even though the effort is maximal. Don’t be alarmed if a 30-second hill sprint only pushes you to, say, 85% MHR – you were limited by time, not effort. In those cases, rely on perceived effort; your HR will accumulate over successive reps. Generally, though, for intervals of 2–5 minutes, expect to spend a lot of time in the 90%+ MHR zone, feeling that burning legs and lungs sensation . Between reps, your HR will drop during recovery jogs, but try not to start the next rep until you’ve come back down to a reasonable level (e.g. under 70% MHR) so you can hit the next one hard. Pro tip: Because HR responds with a slight delay, focus on running the prescribed pace or effort for VO₂ intervals and use HR data more for post-workout analysis. But as a ballpark: if you never exceed ~90% MHR in what was supposed to be a VO₂max session, you might not be pushing hard enough or long enough in the intervals.

Race-Pace Workouts (RPE 8 – specific race pace training): “Race pace” can vary widely depending on the race distance, so make sure to clarify the context. Assuming this refers to training at your goal race pace for a mid-length race (e.g. 10K or half-marathon pace work), the intensity is around RPE 8 – hard but not an all-out sprint. For many, this is near threshold or slightly above. For a 10K race-pace effort, heart rate typically falls around 90% MHR (85–95% range) – essentially on par with or just above lactate threshold. In fact, elite runners often find their 10K race HR is ~90% of max on average . For a shorter race like a 5K, race-pace work is even higher: ~95% MHR during the 5K effort (since a 5K is usually run at VO₂max intensity for ~15–20 minutes). For a longer race like a half marathon, race pace HR will be a bit lower, perhaps ~80–88% MHR (high Zone 3 to low Zone 4), because the distance is longer (half-marathon pace is often just below threshold for advanced runners). Novice runners might hit relatively higher HR for a given race pace because they lack efficiency – e.g. a novice running 10K pace could be near 95–100% MHR by the end of 10K. Trained runners can hold a hard pace at a controlled ~85–90% MHR for longer. Rule of thumb: if you are doing intervals or tempo runs at “race pace,” aim for the HR that you expect to average in that race. For instance, if you know (from past races or estimates) that your 10K average HR is 175 bpm, practice some intervals at roughly that HR. Use caution with HR in race-pace workouts, though – factors like heat or stress can raise it on a given day. It’s okay to slightly adjust the effort so you’re in the right zone. Ultimately, race-pace sessions straddle the line between threshold and VO₂max zones: around 85–95% MHR depending on race length . They teach your body (and mind) to sustain a hard pace comfortably.

Marathon Pace (sustained MP blocks, RPE ~6–7): Marathon pace is a “comfortably hard” pace you can sustain for a very long duration. In terms of HR, marathon intensity is moderate-vigorous but below threshold. For most runners, marathon HR falls in the range of ~70–85% of MHR for the bulk of the race . Staying in this aerobic zone helps avoid early fatigue. “Most experts recommend aiming for ~65–80% of max HR during the marathon” to avoid burning out , though elite competitors racing for the win might push closer to 85%+ in the later miles . In training, when you do marathon-pace workouts (e.g. running a few miles at goal MP), you should target a similar HR zone. For novice or recreational marathoners, marathon pace might only be ~70% MHR (because they’ll be out there for 4–5+ hours and need to conserve energy) . For a well-trained marathoner, goal MP might correspond to ~80–85% MHR (they can sustain a higher effort for ~3 hours). Example: An advanced runner might plan to hold ~80% MHR (say 150 bpm if max is 188) as their average on race day – so their MP training runs should hover around the mid-150s bpm. By contrast, a first-timer might stick to 130–135 bpm if their max is 185 (about 70–73%) to ensure they don’t crash later. It’s individual – one experienced marathoner noted that ~65–70% MHR is his personal “sweet spot” for steady pacing without hitting the wall , but others aiming for aggressive time goals might hold higher. During marathon-pace training blocks, aim for a controlled steady heart rate in high Zone 2 to mid Zone 3 (roughly 75–85% of LTHR, or ~70–80% HRR). You should feel like you could almost converse in short sentences – definitely working, but not as hard as true threshold. If your HR in a marathon-pace run starts creeping toward 90% MHR, that’s a red flag that the pace might be unsustainable over 26 miles. Adjust effort accordingly. The goal is to train your body to be super efficient at that steady effort. By practicing holding your HR in the target zone for longer blocks (e.g. 6–10 miles at MP in training), you’ll learn what marathon effort feels like and avoid nasty surprises on race day.

Interval Repeats (short, fast intervals, RPE 9+): This category can overlap with VO₂ max intervals, but “general speed intervals” might include shorter reps meant to build speed and economy (like 200m repeats, fartleks, etc.). The intended intensity is very high (RPE 9 or even 10), but because some intervals are brief, the heart rate response can vary. For longer intervals (2–3+ minutes), use the VO₂ max guidance above (95%+ MHR). For short sprint intervals (30s to 1min), recognize that HR is a lagging indicator – you might not reach max HR in a 30s sprint, but the goal is still maximal effort. In a set of short repeats, your peak HR might only hit ~85–90% MHR by the end of each rep, then drop during the rest, etc. That’s okay; you’re training neuromuscular speed and anaerobic power. Over the course of many repeats, fatigue will drive the heart rate up eventually. So, for “intervals” workouts, don’t chase a specific HR number during each rep. Instead, use HR to ensure you’re recovering enough between reps and to observe overall intensity. For instance, if doing 10×200m sprints, you might let your HR come down to <60% MHR between reps (so you’re recovered enough to sprint again). During the rep, go all-out (RPE 10) – your HR might only climb to 85% in those 30 seconds, but that’s fine. If the intervals are longer (e.g. 8×400m with 1min rest), you will accumulate toward the high HR zone. By the last few, you might see 90–95% MHR peaks. In summary: Intervals generally target Zone 5 (90%+ MHR) for the effort parts, but the exact HR at each moment is less important than the effort. Use your heart rate more as a gauge of recovery – e.g. if by the last couple reps your HR isn’t dropping below 70% before the next go, you might be reaching your limit. High HR during intervals is expected; just be cautious if you approach 100% MHR and still have many reps left – that might indicate not enough rest or starting too fast.

Progressive Runs (progressing from easy to fast finish, RPE 3 → 8): A progressive run will span multiple zones as you gradually increase the pace. Early on, HR should be in your easy zone (60–70% MHR) and by the end it may climb to threshold zone (85%+ MHR). For example, a runner might start at 65% MHR and finish the last fast miles at ~88% MHR – this is a typical progression where the final effort feels like a tempo run . One sample progressive workout might be 5 miles starting very easy and ending at near 10K race effort. Heart rate in mile 1 might average 130 bpm (easy), and by the last fast mile climb to 170+ (approaching that runner’s threshold). The goal is a smooth increase without a sudden spike – you’re teaching the body to handle increased effort as fatigue accumulates. Novice runners should be careful not to start too fast – the “progression” should truly begin from an easy intensity (for a novice, that could be 60% MHR at the start) so that there’s room to push HR higher later. Elites might start at, say, 70% and finish at 90% MHR; novices might start at 55% and finish at 80% and feel equally challenged. Watch that the ending HR of a progressive run isn’t much above your lactate threshold HR – typically you finish around threshold (RPE 7–8) . If you inadvertently hit VO₂-max-level HR (e.g. 95% MHR) at the end, you may have overshot – the workout morphs into an interval/speed session rather than a controlled progression. Progressive runs are great because they train you to speed up when already tired. Heart-rate wise, think of it as transitioning from Zone 2 up to Zone 4 by the finish. A real example: an experienced runner’s 4-mile progressive tempo started at ~85% and ended at 88% MHR – solidly in threshold zone by the end, but not an all-out sprint. Plan your run so that each mile or segment your HR is a bit higher than the last, culminating in that RPE 8-ish range.

Mixed Workouts (e.g. Threshold + Hills combos): Mixed workouts combine multiple elements, so target heart rates will vary throughout the session. For instance, a workout might include a tempo run followed by hill repeat sprints. In the threshold portion, follow the guidance for threshold runs (~85–90% MHR during those tempo segments). Then, in the hill repeat portion, you’ll spike the intensity – likely into the 90%+ MHR range on each hill (similar to intervals). The key with mixed workouts is to handle the transitions: after the steady threshold part, your HR will be elevated (perhaps ~170 bpm, for example). When you hit a short hill hard, you might go from 170 → 180+ bpm (nearing max). Be mindful of recovery: if the workout intends for you to alternate, say, 5 minutes at threshold then a 30s hill, ensure you jog easy after the hill until your HR comes back down toward the threshold zone before starting the next threshold segment. Otherwise, you’ll carry fatigue and HR too high and turn the threshold segments into something harder than threshold. Novice runners may find their HR stays high once spiked – they should take extra recovery so the threshold work isn’t done above their actual threshold HR. Advanced runners can recover more quickly and may handle the oscillation better. Essentially, a mixed workout will touch Zone 4 (threshold) and Zone 5+ (near max on hills). There isn’t a single “target HR” for the whole workout; instead target the appropriate HR in each block: when it’s threshold block, lock into ~85–90% MHR, and when it’s hill sprint time, give full gas (HR will rise accordingly). This trains your body to change gears. Tip: Don’t be alarmed if by the later sets your heart rate doesn’t fully drop during recoveries – mixed workouts are demanding. But still try to start each rep or segment at a reasonable HR (for threshold segments, ideally back down under 80% before ramping up again if possible). Mixed sessions are advanced; use both HR and RPE to gauge them. If your heart rate in the “tempo” parts keeps drifting upward above your known threshold, consider adjusting the intensity or duration so you maintain the quality of each section.

Hill Repeats (RPE 9–10 on the uphill portions): Hill repeats are essentially a form of high-intensity interval, often focusing on power and form more than heart-rate targeting. During a hard uphill repeat, you will likely reach near-maximal HR by the top of longer hills. For longer hill repeats (1–3 minutes uphill), expect heart rate to climb into the 90–100% MHR zone by the end of each rep (similar to VO₂ max intervals) . Hills tax your cardiovascular system and recruit more muscle, so you might actually hit max HR on a hill easier than on flat intervals – your body is working hard against gravity. For short hill sprints (e.g. 10–20 second explosive hills), your HR won’t spike fully in such a short time; it might only go to 70–80% of max by the time you stop. That’s fine – those workouts are about anaerobic power. In that case, don’t use heart rate as a measure of the effort during the sprint (it’s not fast enough to reflect the intensity), but do watch it in recovery. Allow almost full recovery (heart rate back to a low zone, say <60% MHR) between short hill sprints, since they are usually done with near-max efforts and long rests to build power. For longer hill repeats, you might use a work:rest ratio (e.g. 2:1 or 1:1) and your HR may still be somewhat elevated when you start the next rep – that’s intentional to build stamina. Overall, treat hill repeats like very intense intervals: push hard (HR will rise to the 90%+ zone), then recover thoroughly. If you notice your heart rate doesn’t recover as much after successive hills, it’s a sign of accumulating fatigue – possibly cut the session or extend the rest. One interesting aspect: some runners find their max HR on an uphill is a few beats higher than on flat ground, presumably due to the extra muscle activation and strain. So don’t be surprised if you see numbers at or above your previously observed max during a killer hill rep. In summary: on hills, effort is king. Go by RPE on the climb (it should feel like a 9 or 10 out of 10). Use heart rate mainly to ensure you’re recovered enough to hit the next one with good form. When in doubt, give yourself a bit more recovery, since the goal is quality power output, not just cumulative fatigue.
