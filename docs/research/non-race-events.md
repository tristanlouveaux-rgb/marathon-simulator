1) Periodization & Training Cycles — What Science Says

A large systematic review of elite endurance runners confirms that periodized training — structured in cycles with build and easy weeks — is a key component of performance development, compared to non-periodized constant plans.  

Moreover, world-class endurance coaches across Olympic sports consistently apply a traditional periodization model (macro → meso → micro cycles), typically with easy load weeks every 3-4 weeks to manage fatigue.  

This gives strong scientific justification for your rolling 4–6 week cycles with deliberate recovery weeks built in (e.g., a deload or benchmark test week).
Key principles:
	•	Microcycles: ~1 week
	•	Mesocycles: ~3–6 weeks, focused on specific adaptations
	•	Macrocycles: ~months/long-range view (in non-event mode mainly tracking trends)

This maps directly to your proposed approach of 4-week training cycles:
	•	Weeks 1–3: progressive load
	•	Week 4: recovery + benchmark or deload

That’s consistent with both practice and evidence.

2) Load Progression & Supercompensation

The classic supercompensation model (training → recovery → growth) is foundational in exercise science. You must plan recovery periods because adaptation happens after load, not during it.  

In practical terms:
	•	Progressive overload should be applied thoughtfully
	•	Too much load without recovery increases injury/overreach risk
	•	A deload week enhances adaptation and enables true performance gain

This provides a firm physiological basis for:
✔ load + recovery cycles
✔ benchmark weeks instead of race countdowns
✔ adaptive adjustment of subsequent cycles based on user fatigue response

3) Intensity Distribution in High-Performance Runners

Scientific review of elite runner training shows:
	•	Most successful runners adopt a pyramidal distribution (majority low-intensity, some threshold + some high intensity) in preparatory phases
	•	Some adopt a polarized distribution (lots of low intensity bookended by strategically placed high-intensity sessions) depending on event and focus.  

This suggests that a 2D load model (aerobic vs anaerobic) is meaningful — but not sufficient by itself:
	•	The ratio of intensity matters (e.g., tempo vs VO₂ intervals vs easy)
	•	Your system already captures this via anaerobic/aerobic splits + vibe matching

So yes: you can justify tracking load in two dimensions, but also include a third axis such as LT focus or threshold load because:
	•	Lactate threshold zones are strongly correlated with endurance performance improvements
	•	Threshold training affects performance differently than sub-threshold or VO₂max work, even with matched total load

In other words, purely aerodynamic/anaerobic binary loads are a great start but not the full picture.

4) Does Periodization Style Matter?

Research comparing block periodization vs traditional periodization (both with progressive loading) suggests no clear superiority of one over the other when total load is matched. In other words:
	•	Consistent progressive load cycling with variation can be just as effective as highly structured blocks
	•	The key is progressively increasing training stress without stagnation

This supports your idea of cycles where load gradually rises and then resets/evaluates, without needing overly elaborate long blocks.

5) VO₂max, LT, and Running Economy

VO₂max training is well-supported by research — interval blocks (e.g., 4 × 4 min at ~90–95% VO₂max) can meaningfully improve aerobic capacity.  
But more importantly, combinations of:
	•	LT / tempo runs
	•	Aerobic base work
	•	VO₂max intervals
	•	Strength / economy drills
lead to broader adaptation than focusing on a single dimension alone.

Jack Daniels’ running philosophy, used widely by coaches, argues for distinct training intensities to improve:
	•	Lactate threshold
	•	VO₂max
	•	Running economy
…not just aerobic load.  

This translates into a more nuanced plan generator:
	•	focus phases emphasizing different physiological drivers rather than just “run harder or longer”
	•	benchmarks that measure not only predicted race times, but LT pace and recovery adaptation trends

6) Should You Use a 2D Load Model? (Yes — but extend)

2D is a good foundation, but we recommend a 3-component model for better scientific alignment:
	•	Aerobic load — low/mod aerobic stress
	•	Anaerobic load — high-intensity fatigue
	•	Threshold focus — training aimed at improving lactate threshold / moderate load

Why include threshold?
	•	Threshold training has distinct physiological adaptation patterns and recovery costs compared to VO₂max intervals or easy aerobic runs.
	•	Many elite runners schedule a weekly threshold run even when total anaerobic load is moderate.  

Your existing engine already separates aerobic vs anaerobic — consider a lightweight third axis for LT or tempo work (e.g., via training intensity distribution data) if your data inputs support it.

Key Research-Backed Principles for your System

🔹 Progressive Overload + Recovery
	•	Increase load gradually
	•	Include recovery / deload weeks
	•	Monitor chronic vs acute load trajectories (similar to CTL concepts)  

🔹 Periodization Model
	•	4–6 week mesocycles are evidence-aligned and practical (supercompensation timelines)
	•	Easy weeks improve long-term gains and minimize overtraining  

🔹 Intensity Distribution
	•	Most adaptations come from a mix of easy aerobic volume + threshold + high intensity
	•	A plan generator must balance all three for robust adaptation and user experience  

🔹 Benchmarking
	•	Benchmarks should not only predict a race time, but also estimate:
	•	lactate threshold shifts
	•	VO₂max proxies
	•	economy improvements
