1. Optimal Block Length

Key findings: Physiological adaptations in endurance training emerge on a multi-week timeline.  Studies show VO₂max can begin to rise in as little as ~3 weeks of training , with further improvements continuing out to 8–12 weeks.  Endurance adaptations build gradually: cardiovascular and blood‐volume gains in the 1–4 week range, mitochondrial and metabolic adaptations by ~4–8 weeks, and notable VO₂max/lactate‐threshold gains around 8–12 weeks .  Block periodization literature (Issurin et al.) typically defines a “block” as ~2–4 weeks focused on a specific stimulus .  Longer unbroken loading (beyond ~6–8 weeks) risks accumulating fatigue and overtraining without a targeted refresher, especially for non‐elite athletes.

Recommendations (block length):
	•	3–4 weeks: Moderate blocks allow repeated stimulus changes.  Many coaches use 3 weeks loading + 1 week easier (4week cycle).
	•	4–6 weeks: Suitable for advanced athletes focusing on one capacity (e.g. base endurance or speed), provided an appropriate recovery week follows. Blocks >6 weeks are uncommon without intervening rest.
	•	Block ordering: Rotate focus each block (see Section 4). Use shorter blocks early in a plan (3–4 wk) and possibly extend to ~6 wk in a later base phase if fatigue is well-managed.

Confidence: Moderate – based on physiological timelines and periodization reviews , with expert consensus.

Translation into system: Structure the year as repeating 3–4 week mesocycles (with one easier week after 2–3 hard weeks).  For example, beginner plans might alternate 3 weeks up /1 down, while advanced athletes can sustain 4–5 week loading followed by a deload.  Each block should emphasize one or two goals (e.g. strength/speed vs endurance).

2. Deload / Recovery Weeks

Key findings: Planned cutback periods help dissipate fatigue and reduce injury risk.  Expert sources suggest a recovery week every ~3–6 weeks of hard training .  In running-specific plans, coaches commonly reduce volume by ~10–20% on a deload week and also dial back intensity (fewer intervals, shorter long run).  Physiological studies on tapering show that larger volume cuts (~40–60%) are needed for maximal performance boost , but these are more relevant to pre-race peaking than routine recovery.  Excessive reductions can under-stimulate adaptations. Moderate reductions (20–30%) appear sufficient to restore readiness without loss of fitness.

Recommendations (deload %):
	•	10–15% drop: Minor deload. Helps mental break but may be insufficient to fully clear fatigue.
	•	20–30% drop: Typical recovery week. For example, shorten long run (–10–20%), cut total weekly mileage ~25%, remove one hard session or replace it with easy work . Intensity (pace of workouts) also eases.
	•	40%+ drop: Aggressive taper. Suitable only occasionally (e.g. before an important race). Will clear most fatigue and boost performance , but is overkill for routine blocks.

Confidence: Moderate – based on coach practice and taper meta-analysis .  Evidence supports large cuts for peaking, but recovery weeks aim for smaller reductions.

How it translates: After each training block (e.g. 3–4 weeks of buildup), schedule a lighter week reducing volume by ~20–30% and skipping/or easing hard workouts.  For example, if peak mileage was 40 mi/ wk, drop to ~30 mi (–25%).  Maintain some intensity (e.g. short strides or light intervals) but with reduced volume.  This approach lowers injury risk while preserving fitness.  Depending on the athlete’s response, deloads can be every 4th week or inserted earlier for very fatigued or injury-prone athletes.

3. Progressive Overload Across Blocks

Key findings: Training load should rise gradually to build fitness without injury.  Observational studies in runners find that sudden spikes (>30% increase over 2 weeks) elevate injury risk .  A safe guideline is to keep weekly mileage increases to ~5–10%.  For block-level planning, this translates to roughly a 20–30% total increase in load from one block to the next for novices.  Advanced athletes may handle slightly higher loads but still face diminishing returns and injury risk as loads climb.  The “10% rule” (10% per week) is not scientifically fixed, but provides a rough ceiling.

Recommendations (progression rate):
	•	Weekly increase: Aim for ~5–10% per week (e.g. adding 1–2 mi on a 20–40 mi base).
	•	Per block increase: 15–30% higher load than the prior block (depending on profile).  For example, if a 4-week block averaged 100 km, target ~115–130 km for the next block. Do not exceed ~30% jump in 2 weeks .
	•	Monitoring: Use chronic workload tracking (e.g. 4-week rolling average) to prevent sudden jumps.

Confidence: Moderate – based on injury studies and coaching consensus (no RCTs).

Translation: Plan incremental load rises each block. Beginners/Returning: +5–10% per week (~20% per block). Advanced: can push slightly more (up to ~15% wkly, ~30% block) but watch for fatigue. Use the system’s load mult factors (see cross-training table) to keep total stress in check. Adjust intensities so that key workouts progress (e.g. adding intervals or length to a tempo run) within safe limits.

4. Rotating Training Focus

Key findings: Block periodization (alternating focus) appears more effective than trying to train all qualities equally.  A systematic review found block-periodized programs produced larger VO₂max improvements than traditional all-in-one plans .  This aligns with theory: concentrated blocks target specific adaptations (e.g. speed/power vs endurance base) and avoid interference of conflicting stimuli.  Conversely, a purely linear approach (long undifferentiated buildup) can dilute stimulus and accumulate fatigue.  Classic models (Issurin et al.) recommend concentrating on one or two abilities per block (e.g. focus on LT/threshold work for several weeks, then on VO₂max effort, then on race-specific pace).

Recommendations (periodization):
	•	Block vs linear: Use block periodization. Allocate distinct phases (e.g. base endurance, speed/VO₂, race-pace) each lasting a block of ~3–6 weeks.
	•	Rotating emphasis: For example: Block A – emphasize high-volume/low-intensity (aerobic base); Block B – emphasize VO₂max intervals (speed); Block C – emphasize lactate-threshold/tempo work; then repeat.
	•	Balance: Always include some maintenance of other qualities (e.g. include easy runs during a speed block), but the key workouts shift focus each block.

Confidence: Moderate – systematic reviews show favorable (though limited) evidence for block vs mixed training .  Some advocates (Issurin, Millán, Daniels) strongly endorse it, though there’s debate.

How it translates: Design training blocks with a primary goal (speed, endurance, or mixed).  For instance, month 1 might include 1–2 VO₂max sessions/wk, month 2 switches to threshold workouts, month 3 to long steady runs or hills.  This “concentrated loading” should produce stronger adaptations in each domain rather than a diluted approach.  Ensure recovery weeks between blocks.  The system should tag each block by focus (speed vs endurance) and adjust workout selection and intensity accordingly.

5. Benchmark Testing Frequency & Type

Key findings: Performance testing provides feedback but itself incurs fatigue.  While formal studies on test frequency are scarce, coaches typically test at block endpoints (e.g. every 4–8 weeks).  Common tests: 5K or 3K time trials, 20-min all-out threshold test, or submaximal lactate threshold tests.  Frequent full-effort TTs (weekly) can hinder recovery, so they are best spaced out.  Low-fatigue options (e.g. a 6-min “hard ladder” or an uphill tempo run) can be used more often.  Meta-analyses show time-trial performance improves after proper training and taper, but best practice is to test only as often as to guide adjustments (not every week) .

Recommendations (testing cadence):
	•	Core tests: Every 4–8 weeks (e.g. at the end of a training block) perform a benchmark such as a 20-min threshold run or a 5K TT.
	•	Low-fatigue tests: Alternatively, use shorter maximal efforts (e.g. 3–6 min all-out) or lab/submax tests monthly.
	•	Avoid over-testing: Limit full TTs to perhaps 3–5 times/year (once per quarter or key phase). Between, use training data (paces, heart rate) as proxies.

Confidence: Emerging – based on coaching practice and logic (no direct RCT evidence).

Translation: Build testing into the program schedule, typically at the end of blocks or phases. For example, after a 4-week endurance block, do a controlled 5K time trial to assess fitness changes. Use the results to recalibrate training zones (threshold pace, etc.) but avoid testing so often that it impairs recovery. In the system, categorize tests as “optional benchmarks” and flag them on the calendar roughly every 1–2 blocks for interested athletes.

6. Maintenance Phase Science

Key findings: When athletes reduce or stop training, fitness declines progressively.  Aerobic capacity begins to drop in ~2 weeks of near-total rest , with more pronounced losses by 4–8 weeks.  However, minimal training can greatly slow this regression.  Research indicates as little as two high-intensity workouts per week is enough to maintain VO₂max for ~15 weeks .  In practice, elite athletes often adopt a light “maintenance” mode during off-season or mid-year breaks, keeping frequency/intensity but cutting volume ~50%.  The maintenance threshold is roughly 40–60% of peak workload; going below ~40% for many weeks leads to tangible fitness loss.

Recommendations (maintenance):
	•	Maintenance load: After a peak, sustain at least ~30–50% of peak training load to hold fitness. E.g. if marathon training hit 70 mi/wk, maintain ~30–40 mi with retained intensity.
	•	Minimal sessions: Schedule 2–3 quality workouts (intervals or hard hill runs) plus 1–2 easy runs per week to preserve aerobic adaptations.
	•	Duration: With this reduced program, athletes can maintain most fitness for 2–3 months .

Confidence: Strong – based on detraining studies and guidelines .

How it translates: Implement a “sustain” mode when athletes are not aiming for improvement (e.g. winter base after season).  In this phase, lower overall volume (e.g. 60% of peak) but keep some intensity.  For example, replace one interval day with a moderate tempo, and shorten long run by ~30%.  In the system, treat this as a special block type (e.g. “maintenance mesocycle”) where focus is on recovery and injury prevention rather than progressive overload.

7. Cross-Training & Injury Prevention

Key findings: Non-running aerobic exercise can partly substitute for running, reducing impact stress while retaining fitness.  Classic reviews (Tanaka 1994) show partial transfer of VO₂max across modalities: cycling transfers ~60–70% to running, swimming only ~10–20% .  This aligns with the provided transfer table (e.g. elliptical 65% of run effect).  Mixed-modality training can lower injury risk: studies in athletes indicate that incorporating cross training (like cycling or elliptical) can maintain cardiovascular fitness during injury or heavy load periods, preventing overuse stress on legs .  However, specificity matters: running economy and strength adaptations are best achieved by running itself.  Cross-training should be scaled (using transfer factors) and not exceed capacities – e.g. a hard cycling session counts ~0.75–0.9× of running load.

Recommendations (cross-training):
	•	Load equivalency: Use transfer factors (e.g. elliptical ~0.8× run, cycling ~0.75×, rowing ~0.85×) to equate cross sessions to run miles. For example, a 10 km easy cycle ride (~1hr) might count as ~7–8 km run-equivalent.
	•	Frequency: Substitute cross workouts primarily when injured or to vary stimulus. Up to ~20–30% of weekly volume can safely come from cross modalities without sacrificing adaptation .
	•	Recovery cost: Acknowledge some cross activities (basketball, soccer) add neuromuscular load – adjust recovery accordingly.

Confidence: Moderate – based on exercise physiology reviews .

How it translates: In planning, allow cross-training days by converting cross-sport volume via the table factors (see provided system values).  For example, replacing a run day with cycling would multiply the planned “run miles” by 0.75.  Ensure cross sessions are easy to moderate intensity to avoid new fatigue.  This approach lets runners reduce injury risk (by reducing impact) while still “counting” toward aerobic load.  The system should treat cross workouts as partial substitutes (not full replacements) for run volume, using the transfer table.

👉 Recommended Continuous Training Block Model

Overview: The year-round plan consists of repeating mesocycles (blocks) of ~3–5 weeks of building stress followed by 1 deload week. Each block has a distinct focus (e.g. base endurance, speed, mixed) and gradually increases load by ~15–25% (for novices) or ~25–35% (for advanced). Deloads reduce load by ~20–30%. Testing is done every 4–8 weeks. Over a year (52 weeks), this yields ~10–12 such blocks with progressive volume peaks and scheduled ease.

Profiles: Adjust parameters by athlete level:
	•	Total Beginner/Returning: Very gradual builds (≤5–10% wkly increases), frequent recovery weeks (every 3 wk), block length ~3 weeks. Heavy use of cross-training or walk/jog to manage injury.
	•	Novice/Intermediate: Moderate progressions (10% wkly), blocks of 4 weeks (3 up + 1 down), mixed focus blocks (some speed, some endurance). Benchmark test ~quarterly.
	•	Advanced/Competitive: Faster progression (up to 15% wkly, 30–40% per block), blocks of 4–6 weeks, including specialized speed or threshold phases. Deloads every 4–5 wk. Testing twice per season (mid and end of cycle).
	•	Hybrid (multi-sport): Integrate other activities using transfer factors. Plan block focus on primary sport (running) while maintaining intensity in secondary sports.
	•	All levels: Ensure at least one full rest or easy day per week. In deload weeks, use cross-training at low intensity if desired to replace running while still allowing recovery.

Implementation: In practice, a 12-week program might be:
Block 1 (Weeks 1–4): Build Endurance (increase weekly volume ~20%, include 1–2 easy tempo runs).
Week 5: Recovery (–25% volume, no hard workouts).
Block 2 (6–9): Build Speed (focus VO₂max intervals, maintain base runs).
Week 10: Recovery.
Block 3 (11–14): Build Threshold (long tempos, moderate long run pace).
Week 15: Recovery & Testing (e.g. 5K TT or 20-min test).
Then repeat/develop.

This scalable model ensures continuous adaptation without plateau: each block pushes fitness, each deload prevents overtraining, and focus-shifting yields balanced development. Advanced runners carry higher volumes and intensities than beginners, but all follow the same structural principles. Over 4–52 weeks, athletes progress through multiple cycles, with parameters tuned to their profile (as above). The plan can be coded into the system by cycling through these blocks, using the cross-training equivalency table and Garmin data to adjust load for individual athletes.

Confidence: Overall the recommendations draw on strong principles of exercise physiology and existing literature (VO₂/detraining studies , training-injury research , taper meta ). Some specifics (e.g. exact % deload or block length) rely on emerging consensus and coaching practice. The suggested model should be adjusted per individual response, but provides an evidence-informed framework for year-round endurance training.
